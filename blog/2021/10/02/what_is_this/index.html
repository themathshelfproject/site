What is this?

Answers to all your WTFs

Published

02 October 2021

As a kid, I always had a thing for books. Physical books that is, not the stuff from libgen we cram our hard drives with these days. I loved running my hands over hardcovers, emulating the classic Rory Gilmore pose with paperbacks, and pretty much doing everything with books other than actually reading them.

I know - weird.

My parents assumed I’d outgrow it, but I didn’t. Instead, I became a tween hoarder, building my own Library of Alexandria with whatever print material I could possibly get my hands on. This mania continued until the 10th grade, when, for some unknown-to-this-day reason, I started focusing my collection on math texts.

I wasn’t particularly good at math, and there was no way in hell I could be convinced to sit down and actually read books on the subject, but I was still obsessed with them. However, due to cabal that is mathematics publishing, they were way outside my price range.

Enter Amazon - and the strangest hobby of all time.

In my final two years of high school, I clocked well over $500$ hours reading reviews of math books I wanted to buy on amazon.com. I knew that Baby Rudin, Munkres, and Dummit & Foote could be digitally had in a few clicks, but I had no intention of actually assessing these texts. Rather, I spent hours every day imagining what it would feel like to have read these tomes cover to cover.

I know, I know - this is pathological.

Fortunately however, my parents, who had grown increasingly suspicious of my screen time, were convinced that I was either running the Silk Road from my bedroom or threatening national security. Both would have been problematic for their reputation, so I was shipped off to India the summer before college to spend time with my luddite grandparents. I didn’t see it as such back then, but in retrospect, it was a blessing in disguise.

In college, I studied computer science along with a touch of mathematics: multivariable calculus, linear algebra, discrete math, and elementary number theory (i.e. the standard fare). Ironic? Absolutely. But nothing life-changing - or at least not enough to get me to start a math blog. Besides the jarring experience of having to prove things rather than just plug and chug, these courses were pretty meh.

Nevertheless, there was one benefit; the sheer workload of a CS degree and constantly walking across a $1200$ acre campus kept me far too busy to explore the Amazon math catalog.

Then it happened.

Immigrant Indian parents have a strange fetish for AP classes, so I had lots of general education credits by the time I got to campus. As a result, I had a light schedule - perhaps too light - planned for the spring semester of my senior year. Further, I had heard that graph theory was useful for programming interviews (total catfish by the way), so I decided to sign up for CMSC 475 with this man, who happened to have good reviews. However, what I had failed to discover was that he was a former IMO silver medalist with a penchant for contest-style combinatorics problems.

Fuck.

True to his credentials, Dr. Ebrahimian gave out hard - no, brutal - problem sets. However, to my utter surprise, I actually enjoyed doing them, so much so that I started looking forward to the homework. For the first time in my life, I liked actually doing math a lot more than imagining myself having mastered it. The process of playing around with a problem, hitting a wall, putting the pieces together - it was all inexplicably addictive.

However, just as I was getting to used to the idyllic math lifestyle, I graduated - and was promptly thrust into the big, bad world. My parents had invested many zeroes into my education, and I had always embodied the debt repayment policy of the Lannisters.

These days, I’m a software engineer with a comfortable job and an empty bookshelf. For better or worse (but probably worse), I’d like to build the math collection of my teenage dreams. However, this time, I’m going to do it right; a textbook only goes on the shelf once I’ve solved every single problem in it.

Will I fill the shelf? Maybe, maybe not. But at least I’ll enjoy the journey.
