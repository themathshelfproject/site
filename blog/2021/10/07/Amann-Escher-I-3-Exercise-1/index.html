I.3 Exercise 1

Published

07 October 2021

Problem

Let $f: X \to Y$, $g: Y \to V$ be bijective. Then $g \circ f: X \to V$ is bijective and

$$ (g \circ f)^{-1} = f^{-1} \circ g^{-1} $$

Solution

Lemma I: $(g \circ f): X \to V$ is injective

Proof:

Let $x_1, x_2$ be arbitrary elements of $X$ such that

$$ (g \circ f)(x_1) = (g \circ f)(x_2) = v \in V $$

Then, by the definition of composition,

$$ \begin{align*} (g \circ f)(x_1) = g(f(x_1)) = v = g(f(x_2)) = (g \circ f)(x_2) \end{align*} $$

Since, $f, g$ are both bijective, they are both injective. Therefore,

$$ \begin{align*} g(f(x_1)) = g(f(x_2)) \implies f(x_1) = f(x_2) \implies x_1 = x_2 \end{align*} $$

This completes the lemma.


Lemma II: $(g \circ f): X \to V$ is surjective

Proof:

Let $v$ be an arbitrary element of $V$. Since $f, g$ are both bijective, they are also surjective. Therefore, there exist $y \in Y$ and $x \in X$ such that $g(y) = v$ and $f(x) = y$.

Hence, by the definition of composition.

$$ \begin{align*} (g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x)) = g(y) = v \end{align*} $$

Now, observe that since the choice of $v$ was arbitrary, we can conclude, without loss of generality, that for any $v \in V$ there exists some $x \in X$ such that $(g \circ f)(x) = v$. This completes the lemma.


Therefore, by Lemmas I and II, $(g \circ f): X \to V$ is bijective.



We now show the second claim.

Since $f$, $g$, and $g \circ f$ are all bijective, we know that the inverse mappings

$$ \begin{align*} &f^{-1}: Y \to X \\ &g^{-1}: V \to Y \\ &(g \circ f)^{-1}: V \to X \end{align*} $$

are all well-defined. Then, by the definition of composition,

$$ f^{-1} \circ g^{-1}: V \to X $$

Hence, $f^{-1} \circ g^{-1}$ and $(g \circ f)^{-1}$ have the same domain ($V$) and codomain ($X$). All that remains is to show that they have the same rule. For this, let $v$ be an arbitrary element of $V$ and recall from Lemma II that $g \circ f$ is surjective. Therefore, there exists some $x \in X$ such that $(g \circ f)(x) = v$


Lemma III: $g^{-1}(v) = f(x)$

Proof:

By the definition of composition,

$$ (g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x)) = v $$

Also, since $g \circ g^{-1} = id_{V}$ by the definition of inverse mappings,

$$ g(g^{-1}(v)) = v $$

Since $g$ is bijective, it is also injective. Thus, we have $g^{-1}(v) = f(x)$ as desired.


Now,

$$ \begin{alignat}{2} (f^{-1} \circ g^{-1})(v) &= f^{-1}(g^{-1}(v)) &&\text{ (by definition of composition)} \\ &= f^{-1}(f(x)) &&\text{ (by lemma III)} \\ &= x &&\text{ (since } (f^{-1} \circ f = id_{X}) \text{ )} \\ &= (g \circ f)^{-1}((g \circ f)(x)) &&\text{ (since } ((g \circ f)^{-1} \circ (g \circ f) = id_{X}) \text{ )} \\ &= (g \circ f)^{-1}(v) &&\text{ (since } (g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x)) = v \text{ )} \end{alignat} $$

Therefore, the rule is also the same and we are done.
